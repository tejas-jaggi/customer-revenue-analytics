# Phase 5 — Executive Findings Matrix
## Customer Revenue Analytics — Solstice Apparel Co.

Consolidated, validated findings from Sections A–G, with evidence strength and recommended action. This matrix is the primary input to the Phase 5 Executive Synthesis. Every finding below is backed by executed, validation-passing SQL (45/45); evidence strength reflects how directly the data supports the *business conclusion*, not whether the SQL ran.

**Evidence strength key:** **High** = directly measured, reconciles to a certified anchor, robust to basis choice. **Medium** = measured but rests on an analytical assumption or a modest spread. **Low** = modeled/counterfactual or a limitation-bounded inference.

| # | Finding | Section(s) | Evidence | Business Implication | Recommended Executive Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Value is a *frequency* phenomenon.** Repeat customers place 7.52 orders vs 1.0 for one-timers at near-identical AOV ($84 vs $80); the 82.4% repeat-revenue share is driven by return visits, not bigger baskets. | F, A | **High** | The core value lever is retention, not upsell. Basket-building programs are second-order; getting customers to come back is first-order. | Prioritize retention/repeat-purchase programs over AOV-lifting initiatives. Fund lifecycle marketing, replenishment, loyalty. |
| 2 | **Revenue is steeply concentrated.** The Loyal 7+ segment (17% of customers) drives 63% of net revenue; the top revenue decile alone drives 50.1%. | F | **High** | The business depends on a small, high-value core — a concentration *risk* as much as a strength. | Protect the top ~1,500 customers explicitly (retention SLAs, churn monitoring). Treat top-decile attrition as a board-level KPI. |
| 3 | **Controllable returns are the #1 realized value leak** — $307.7K (53.5% of realized leakage, larger than all discounting), of which Wrong Size alone is 40.6%. | G, A | **High** | The single largest operational drain is also the most fixable, and it is a *sizing* problem. | Launch a sizing-accuracy initiative (fit guides, size tools, standardized sizing) as the top operational priority. Size the prize (see #10). |
| 4 | **Footwear is a fit problem; Womenswear is the bigger dollar risk.** Footwear returns 27.8% (54.8% Wrong Size); Womenswear's return *dollar exposure* is $171K — 2× Footwear — despite a lower rate. | G, C | **High** | Rate flags Footwear, dollars flag Womenswear. Both need sizing fixes; the larger cheque is Womenswear. | Apply sizing intervention to Footwear (rate) and Womenswear (exposure) first. Track dollar exposure, not just rate. |
| 5 | **Accessories is the value engine.** #2 in revenue but #1 in profit (70.5% margin), lowest returns (8.6%), #1 in returns-adjusted profit, holds all top-5 profit SKUs. | C | **High** | The most profitable, most return-resilient category is not the revenue leader — an under-leveraged asset. | Expand Accessories assortment and basket-attachment; it compounds favorably on both margin and returns. |
| 6 | **Paid acquisition brings the highest-value customers** — overturning the "paid = volume, owned = loyalty" assumption. Paid Social leads on both volume (2,519) and value ($294/customer); its value edge survives returns intact (17.0% return rate ≈ blended). | E, F, G | **High** | The channels that scale are also the ones bringing durable customers; the value advantage is real, not a returns artifact. | Tilt incremental acquisition spend to Paid Social / Paid Search — *with the caveat that CAC/ROI is unmeasured* (no spend data). Instrument spend before large reallocation. |
| 7 | **Growth deceleration is holiday-specific, not structural.** Annual growth 138%→38%, but December same-month YoY collapsed 129%→8.8% while off-peak July still grew ~33%. | B | **Medium** | Underlying demand is healthy; the 2024 holiday engine plateaued. The slowdown is a seasonal-peak issue, not a demand problem. | Investigate the 2024→2025 holiday plateau (promo/inventory/channel) as a Phase 7 diagnostic — it needs data the warehouse lacks. |
| 8 | **Loyalty is not a return risk.** Loyal customers return at 17.1% (= one-timers); top-decile customers lose only 15.0% of value to returns vs 22.3% for the rest. | G, F | **High** | The best customers are not disproportionately costly; returns-adjusted concentration is even *more* skewed to the top. | Do not hesitate to invest in high-value customers over return concerns — they are net *more* efficient. |
| 9 | **Geography is a weak differentiator.** Regional revenue-per-customer index spans only 94–106; customer quality, channel mix, and return rates are all near-uniform across regions. | D, E, F, G | **High** | Regional strategy is a second-order lever. The West's slight edge is diffuse, not driven by composition, channel, or returns. | De-prioritize geographic segmentation as a strategic axis; concentrate on product and customer dimensions where spreads are wide. |
| 10 | **The retention prize dwarfs every realized leak.** Modeled opportunity cost of one-time customers not reaching average repeat value ≈ $2.7M — ~4× total realized leakage. | G, F | **Low** (modeled counterfactual) | Reframes the strategic hierarchy: fixing returns protects ~$308K; converting one-timers to repeat is a multi-million-dollar prize. | Make second-purchase conversion the flagship strategic goal (90-day repeat rate is only 24.3%). *Treat the $2.7M as directional, not a committed number.* |

## Notes for the synthesis

- **Findings 1, 2, 3 are the spine** — retention is where value lives, the base is concentrated, and returns are the biggest fixable operational leak. The synthesis should lead with these.
- **Finding 10 is the most powerful and the softest.** It is a modeled counterfactual (every one-timer reaching *average* repeat value is optimistic by construction). Carry the "modeled / directional" caveat in the headline, not a footnote — it is quarantined from realized leakage throughout Phase 5 and must stay so.
- **Findings 7 and 6 carry explicit limitations** — the holiday plateau cannot be diagnosed with warehouse data alone (needs promo/inventory), and channel value cannot be confirmed as cost-*efficient* without CAC data. State both limits when these findings are used.
- **The negative findings (9, and the composition non-results behind 6) are strengths** — reached by elimination across multiple sections, they let the synthesis say what does *not* matter with confidence, which sharpens the recommendations that do.
- **A suggested next number for the synthesis (no new SQL required):** size the controllable-returns recovery concretely — e.g. "halving Wrong Size returns recovers ~$84K/year" — so Finding 3 lands as a funded intervention, not a percentage.
